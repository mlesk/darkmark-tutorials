# Mission: Mathematical Competence for an AI-Driven Economy

## Why

To develop sufficient depth in linear algebra and probability that I can identify and evaluate opportunities to apply generative AI in any professional domain — not by building models myself, but by genuinely understanding what these systems do, what they cannot do, where they will fail, and how to think through approaches, challenges, and execution. This is about becoming an AI-literate professional who can bridge between technical reality and strategic decision-making, grounded in the actual mathematics rather than marketing or vibes.

## Success looks like

- Reading a technical AI blog post, paper abstract, or system card and understanding the mathematical claims well enough to judge whether the approach is sound for a given problem
- Reasoning about where an AI system is likely to fail — edge cases, distribution shifts, confidence miscalibration — without running code
- Communicating AI capabilities and limitations clearly to non-technical stakeholders, with the depth to back up every claim
- Recognizing when a problem in a given domain is or isn't amenable to an AI approach, and articulating why in terms of the underlying math (representation, uncertainty, optimization)

## Constraints

- Self-paced — quality and depth over speed
- No coding or implementation — conceptual mastery comes first
- No theorem-proving or pure-math rigor — applied competence is the goal
- Calculus beyond single-variable basics is out of scope (only what's needed as a prerequisite for probability and optimization concepts)

## Out of scope

- Building or training ML models in code
- Mathematical proofs and pure-math formalism
- Advanced calculus (multivariable, differential equations, real analysis)
- Specific ML frameworks, tools, or engineering practices
- Any single domain application — the goal is transferable mathematical literacy, not domain expertise
