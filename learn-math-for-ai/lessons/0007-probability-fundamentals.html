0007 — Probability: The Language of Uncertainty

Probability Pillar · ~20 minutes

🧩 Puzzle Piece: A transformer doesn't output "the next word." It outputs a probability distribution over 50,000+ possible next tokens. When GPT says "I'm 73% sure the next word is 'the'," that's not a metaphor — it's a literal probability, computed through the softmax function. To understand what a transformer actually produces, you need to understand probability.
AI systems deal with uncertainty. Which token comes next? Is this image a cat or a dog? Will this user click? Probability is the mathematical framework for reasoning about uncertainty — and it's the native language of every generative AI system.

The Three Axioms of Probability

All of probability theory rests on three axioms (Kolmogorov, 1933):

  1. Non-negativity: P(A) ≥ 0 for any event A. Probabilities can't be negative.
  2. Unit measure: P(Ω) = 1. The probability of something happening is 1.
  3. Additivity: If A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B).

That's it. Everything else — Bayes' theorem, expectation, distributions, entropy — follows from these three rules.

Sample Space and Events

The sample space Ω is the set of all possible outcomes. For a language model, Ω is the vocabulary — every token the model can produce. An event is any subset of Ω. P(event) is the total probability mass assigned to outcomes in that event.

Why Probability for AI?

AI systems face three kinds of uncertainty that probability handles naturally:

  1. Aleatoric uncertainty — genuine randomness in the world. Which word comes next in a sentence is inherently uncertain; many continuations are valid. Probability quantifies this.
  2. Epistemic uncertainty — what the model doesn't know. A model trained on limited data should express lower confidence on unfamiliar inputs. Probability provides calibrated confidence scores.
  3. Learning from data — the core of machine learning. "Find the parameters that make the training data most probable" is the maximum likelihood principle behind virtually all generative AI training.
AI without probability is just pattern matching. Probability is what turns "this looks similar to things I've seen" into "I am 73% confident that the next token is 'the', 11% that it's 'a', and 0.01% that it's 'pterodactyl'." The numbers are meaningful — they're calibrated statements about uncertainty.

Two Types of Probability

Frequentist: Probability = long-run frequency. "If I rolled this die infinitely many times, how often would I get a 6?"

Bayesian: Probability = degree of belief. "Given what I know, how confident am I that this email is spam?" Bayesian probability updates beliefs with evidence — it's the framework behind modern AI.

AI uses both views. The training objective is frequentist (maximize likelihood across the dataset). But the model's output — "I'm 73% confident in 'the'" — is a Bayesian statement of belief conditioned on the input. The mathematics handles both seamlessly.

Q1: A language model outputs probabilities for the next token. The probabilities across all possible tokens must satisfy which condition?

A. They must all be equal B. They must be non-negative and sum to exactly 1 C. The highest must be at least 0.5 D. They can be any real numbers

Q2: A model assigns P("the" | context) = 0.73 and P("a" | context) = 0.11. What is P(not "the" and not "a" | context)?

A. 0.16 B. 0.84 C. Cannot determine from this information alone D. 0.08 (the product, since tokens are independent)

Q3: Why is probability the right language for AI output, rather than just picking the single most likely answer?

A. Picking the single most likely answer gives identical results and is simpler B. The probability distribution captures the model's uncertainty — it tells you not just what's most likely, but how much more likely it is than alternatives C. It's required by the GPU hardware D. It makes the output human-readable
