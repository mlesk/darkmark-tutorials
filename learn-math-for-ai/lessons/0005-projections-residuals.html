0005 — Projections, Orthogonality & Residuals

Linear Algebra Pillar · ~25 minutes

🧩 Puzzle Piece: Every transformer block contains residual connections — the "Add" in "Add & Norm." Instead of replacing the input, the block computes a correction and adds it: output = input + block(input). This lesson teaches the linear algebra behind why this works: projections, orthogonal decomposition, and why preserving the original signal while adding a learned correction prevents deep networks from collapsing.
A vector can be split into two parts: the part that lies along another vector (its projection), and the part that's perpendicular (the residual). This decomposition — splitting "what aligns" from "what's left over" — is the mathematical idea behind one of the most important architectural tricks in deep learning.

Projection: The Shadow of One Vector on Another

The projection of vector v onto vector u is the component of v that points in the direction of u:

proju(v) = (v·u / u·u) · u

Geometrically: drop a perpendicular from the tip of v to the line through u. The projection is the vector from the origin to that foot. It's the closest approximation of v that lies entirely along u.

Orthogonal Decomposition

Any vector v can be split into two perpendicular (orthogonal) pieces:

v = proju(v) + (v − proju(v))

The first term is along u (the projection). The second term is perpendicular to u (the residual). These two components are orthogonal — their dot product is zero — meaning they carry completely independent information.

Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero. Orthogonal vectors have no overlap, no redundancy. An orthogonal basis — where every basis vector is perpendicular to every other — is the most efficient coordinate system possible because each dimension captures completely independent information.

Residual Connections: The "Add" in "Add & Norm"

In a transformer block, the output is:

output = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x))

where Sublayer is either attention or the feed-forward network. Instead of output = Sublayer(x) (replacing x entirely), the block computes x + Sublayer(x) — it adds a correction to the original.

Why does this matter?

  1. Gradient flow: During training, the gradient can flow directly through the "+" path, bypassing the sublayer. This prevents the "vanishing gradient" problem in deep networks.
  2. Learning residuals: The sublayer only needs to learn the difference between what the input already encodes and what it should encode. If the identity is already good, the sublayer can output near-zero.
  3. Information preservation: The original signal x is never lost — it's always there, with the sublayer's output added as a refinement.
Residual connections decompose learning into "keep what works, fix what doesn't." Each layer doesn't need to rediscover everything from scratch. It only needs to compute the correction — the residual — and add it. This is why transformers can be stacked 96+ layers deep without the earlier layers' information being washed away.

Layer Normalization

The "Norm" in "Add & Norm" standardizes each vector to have mean 0 and variance 1 (with learned scale and shift parameters). This keeps vector magnitudes from exploding or vanishing as they pass through many layers — a practical necessity when composing dozens of transformations.
