0000 — The Transformer: Your North Star

Precursor · The destination before the journey · ~25 minutes

You already use transformers every day. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — all transformer-based. DALL·E, Midjourney, Sora — built on transformer variants. GitHub Copilot, the assistant writing code with you right now — a transformer. This lesson shows you what's inside the black box. Not the code, not the engineering — the mathematical ideas that make it work. Everything you learn in the next 14 lessons builds toward understanding this architecture from the ground up.

What Does a Transformer Do?

At its core, a transformer takes a sequence of tokens and predicts what comes next.

"The cat sat"
→
Transformer
→
"on" (73%)
"under" (11%)
"." (3%)
…

That's it. Input tokens in, probability distribution over next tokens out. Repeat to generate text: take the predicted token, append it to the input, run the transformer again.

This is the fundamental operation of every large language model. GPT-4, Claude 4, Gemini 2.5 — all of them, at their mathematical core, do exactly this: take a sequence of tokens, output a probability distribution over the next token. The difference between them is scale (billions vs. trillions of parameters), training data, and engineering — not the underlying math.

The Four Mathematical Pillars

The transformer rests on four mathematical ideas. You'll master the first two in depth, and we'll touch the third and fourth as needed:

🧮 Linear Algebra

The language of data representation and transformation

  • Vectors — words, images, users become lists of numbers
  • Matrices — transformations that move vectors between spaces
  • Matrix multiplication — the computational engine of attention
  • Embeddings — learned vector representations that capture meaning
  • SVD / low-rank — how we compress and understand models

🎲 Probability & Information Theory

The language of uncertainty, learning, and measurement

  • Conditional probability & Bayes — learning from evidence
  • Entropy & cross-entropy — how we measure "wrongness"
  • KL divergence — comparing distributions
  • Softmax — the bridge between vector math and probability

What You'll Be Able to Do

After completing this sequence, you will be able to:

  • Read the "Attention Is All You Need" paper with genuine comprehension — every equation, every diagram
  • Evaluate AI claims critically: when someone says "our model uses a novel attention mechanism," you'll know what questions to ask
  • Reason about where transformers will fail: token limits, attention bottlenecks, distribution mismatches
  • Communicate precisely about AI capabilities: "the model outputs a categorical distribution over 50,257 tokens" instead of "it guesses the next word"
  • Bridge between technical teams and decision-makers, with the math to back up every statement
You will not be able to build a transformer. That's not the goal. You'll be able to do something rarer and arguably more valuable: think clearly about what transformers can and cannot do, and explain why, in mathematical terms that are actually correct. In an AI-driven economy, that skill — genuine mathematical literacy without the implementation — is the gap this course fills.
