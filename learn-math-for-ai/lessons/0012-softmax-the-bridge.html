0012 — Softmax: The Bridge Between Worlds

Convergence · ~25 minutes · Where linear algebra meets probability

🧩 Puzzle Piece: Softmax appears twice in the transformer. First, inside attention: softmax(Q·Kᵀ/√dₖ) converts raw similarity scores into attention weights (a probability distribution over which tokens to attend to). Second, at the output: softmax(logits) converts the final vector into a probability distribution over the vocabulary. Softmax is the mathematical function that takes the outputs of linear algebra (dot products, matrix multiplications) and turns them into the language of probability. It is the bridge.
You have a vector of raw numbers: [2.3, −0.5, 0.0, 4.1]. You want a probability distribution: numbers between 0 and 1 that sum to 1, preserving the relative ordering (4.1 should get the most probability, −0.5 the least). How? Softmax.

The Softmax Function

softmax(z)i = exp(zi) / Σⱼ exp(zj)

Step by step:

  1. exp(zᵢ): exponentiate each component. This makes all values positive (eˣ > 0 for all real x).
  2. Σⱼ exp(zⱼ): sum all the exponentiated values. This is the normalization constant.
  3. Divide: each exp(zᵢ) by the sum. This ensures all outputs sum to 1.

The result is a valid probability distribution — every entry is non-negative and they sum to exactly 1.

Softmax is the function that makes attention work. Q·Kᵀ produces a matrix of raw similarity scores — real numbers that could be anything. Softmax converts each row into a probability distribution: "here's how much attention to pay to each token." Without softmax, attention would just be a weighted sum with arbitrary weights. With softmax, it's a weighted average with probabilistically meaningful weights.

Temperature: Controlling Confidence

With a temperature parameter T:

softmax(z/T)i = exp(zi/T) / Σⱼ exp(zj/T)
  • T → 0: softmax approaches argmax — all probability on the largest value. Deterministic, "cold."
  • T = 1: standard softmax. The model's natural confidence.
  • T → ∞: softmax approaches uniform — all tokens equally likely. Maximum randomness, "hot."

This is the "temperature" slider you see in ChatGPT and other LLM interfaces. Lower temperature = more focused, predictable outputs. Higher temperature = more creative, diverse outputs. You're literally adjusting how peaked the probability distribution is.

The Scaling Factor: √dₖ

In the attention formula, softmax gets Q·Kᵀ/√dₖ — the dot products are divided by √dₖ before softmax. Why?

As dₖ (the dimension of key/query vectors) grows, dot products grow in magnitude. Large magnitudes pushed through softmax produce near-one-hot distributions — the model becomes overconfident, and gradients vanish (the softmax saturates). Dividing by √dₖ keeps the variance of the dot products roughly constant regardless of dimension. This is a subtle but critical detail that makes deep transformers trainable.

The √dₖ scaling is a mathematical fix for a practical problem. Without it, large models with high-dimensional keys would produce degenerate attention patterns. With it, attention works across a wide range of model sizes. This kind of detail — understanding not just what the formula is, but why each piece is there — is the essence of mathematical competence.

Softmax in the Full Architecture

Softmax appears at two critical junctions:

  1. Attention weights: softmax(Q·Kᵀ/√dₖ) — converts similarity matrix to attention probabilities. "Which tokens should I pay attention to?"
  2. Output layer: softmax(W_output · h_final) — converts the final hidden state to next-token probabilities. "What's the most likely next word?"

Both are the same function, applied at different scales. The first operates over context positions (1024–128K). The second operates over vocabulary tokens (~50K). Same math, different purpose.
